## Evaluating the Business Plan and Model

Evaluating the business plan and model of a small business you are about to buy is crucial to ensure that the business is viable and profitable. The first step in evaluating the business plan is to review the company's goals and objectives. The goals should be clear, specific, and measurable, and should align with the company's overall mission and vision. The objectives should be broken down into actionable steps that can be tracked and measured over time. If the goals and objectives are not well-defined or realistic, it may be a sign that the business plan is not well thought out.

## Financials, Assets & Liabilities

When evaluating a small business for purchase, it's crucial to review the financials of the company, including expenses, revenue, debts, and assets. Reviewing the financials will give you a clear picture of the company's financial health and will help you make an informed decision about whether or not to buy the business.

### Reviewing Expenses

First, start by reviewing the company's expenses. Look at the monthly and annual expenses, including:

- Rent
- Utilities
- Payroll
- Other operating costs

Determine if these expenses are in line with industry standards and if they are sustainable for the business. If the expenses are high and unsustainable, it may be a sign that the company is not profitable.

### Reviewing Revenue

Next, review the company's revenue. Look at the monthly and annual revenue, including:

- Sales
- Subscriptions
- Other income streams

Determine if the revenue is stable, growing, or declining. If the revenue is declining, it may be a sign that the company is losing market share or facing increased competition.

### Debts and Assets

It's also important to review the company's debts and assets. Look at any outstanding debts, including:

- Loans
- Credit lines
- Accounts payable

Determine if the company has the ability to pay off these debts or if they are at risk of defaulting. Additionally, review the company's assets, including:

- Inventory
- Equipment
- Property

Determine if these assets are valuable and if they are being used effectively to generate revenue.

**Key Takeaway:** Reviewing the finances of a small business is a critical step in evaluating the company's financial health and potential for success. By carefully reviewing the expenses, revenue, debts, and assets, you can make an informed decision about whether or not to purchase the business.

## Market & Competitors

Looking at the market and competition is an important step in evaluating a small business for purchase. To do so, start by researching the industry and market the business operates in. Look at market trends, customer demographics, and any other relevant data that can help you understand the current state of the market.

### Identifying Competitors

Next, identify the primary competitors of the business. Look at their:

- Products or services
- Pricing
- Marketing strategies
- Customer reviews

Determine if the business has a competitive advantage over its competitors or if it is at a disadvantage.

### SWOT Analysis

To gain further insight into the market and competition, consider conducting a SWOT analysis. This involves identifying the:

- **Strengths** - What the business does well
- **Weaknesses** - Areas where the business is vulnerable
- **Opportunities** - External factors the business could capitalize on
- **Threats** - External factors that could harm the business

The analysis can help you identify potential areas for growth and improvement and can also help you understand the risks and challenges the business may face.

## Customers & Employees

These two groups can have a significant impact on the success of the business and should be carefully evaluated.

### Evaluating the Customer Base

When looking at the customer base of the business try to understand their:

- Demographics
- Preferences
- Buying habits

Determine if the business has a loyal customer base or if it is struggling to retain customers. Look at customer feedback and reviews online to get a sense of how customers feel about the business and its products or services.

### Evaluating the Workforce

When reviewing the current workforce of the business determine if it is well-trained, motivated, and productive. Look at:

- Turnover rate of employees
- Reasons why employees are leaving
- Key employees critical to success
- Likelihood they will stay after purchase

You can also review places like Glassdoor to see how employees feel about their employment at the company.

### Company Culture

It's also important to evaluate the culture of the business and determine if it aligns with your values and goals. Look at the leadership style of the current owner and determine if it is effective in motivating employees and driving success.

## Legal Considerations

When evaluating a small business for purchase, conducting a thorough legal review is crucial to ensure that you understand any potential legal liabilities or risks associated with the business. Here are some key areas to focus on:

### Business Structure

Determine if the business is structured as a:

- Sole proprietorship
- Partnership
- LLC
- Corporation

Each structure has different legal and tax implications, so it's important to understand the implications of the current structure and whether it's appropriate for your needs.

### Contracts

Review all contracts that the business has entered into, including:

- Leases
- Vendor agreements
- Customer contracts

Look for any unfavorable terms or potential liabilities.

### Intellectual Property

Determine if the business has any patents, trademarks, or copyrights that are important to its operations. Review any licensing agreements and make sure they are in good standing.

### Compliance with Laws and Regulations

Determine if the business is compliant with all relevant laws and regulations, such as:

- Labor laws
- Environmental regulations
- Tax laws

Look for any potential liabilities that could arise from non-compliance.

### Litigation History

Review any past or pending litigation involving the business. Determine if there are any potential liabilities that could arise from ongoing lawsuits.

**Key Takeaway:** Conducting a thorough legal review is an important step in evaluating a small business for purchase. Working with an experienced attorney can help ensure that you identify any potential legal issues and make an informed decision about whether or not to buy the business.

## Growth Potential

When evaluating a small business for purchase, assessing its growth potential is a critical factor to consider. Here are some key areas to focus on:

### Industry Trends

Determine if the industry in which the business operates is growing or declining. Look at market reports and industry publications to get a sense of the overall market trends and projections. This will help you understand if the business is well-positioned to take advantage of any growth opportunities.

### Competition

Determine who the main competitors are and how the business stacks up against them. Look at the competitive landscape and evaluate the business's unique selling proposition and competitive advantage.

### Customer Base

Determine if the business has a loyal and growing customer base. Look at customer feedback and reviews online to get a sense of how customers feel about the business and its products or services. Consider whether there are opportunities to expand the customer base through targeted marketing or new product offerings.

### Scalability

Determine if the business has the potential to scale operations and increase revenue. Look at the current infrastructure and determine if it can support growth. Consider whether there are opportunities to expand into new markets or offer new products or services.

### Financial Performance

Review the financial performance of the business and evaluate its revenue and profitability trends. Look at the balance sheet and determine if there are any potential financial risks or liabilities.

## Get Outside Help

Before purchasing a business, it is important to consult with several experts in order to make an informed decision. These experts may include:

| Expert | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Attorney** | Review entity documents, bylaws, resolutions, and operating agreements |
| **CPA** | Assess financial health and determine business worth |
| **Insurance Adviser** | Identify potential liabilities associated with the business |
| **Lender** | Provide additional funding if needed |

## Summary

Assessing the growth potential of a small business is important to determine if it's a worthwhile investment. By understanding the industry trends, competition, customer base, scalability, and financial performance, you can make an informed decision about whether the business has the potential to grow and generate a good return on investment.
