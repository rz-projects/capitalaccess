## What Is a Cash Out Refinance?

A cash out refinance is a mortgage loan that allows you to take money out of your home. The amount you can borrow depends on the value of your home and other factors, such as credit score and income. You can use this money for any reason--it doesn't have to be used for home improvements or repairs like with an equity loan or home equity line of credit (HELOC).

## Types of Cash Out Refinances

There are two main types:

### Conventional Cash-Out Refinance

Conventional cash-out refinances allow borrowers to borrow up to 80% LTV (loan-to-value), which means they're taking on more debt than they did when they first purchased their property. This type is also known as "80/20" because it allows them access up until 80% LTV at closing; however, they'll need another 20% down payment in order meet requirements set by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guidelines.

### Jumbo Cash-Out Refinance

Jumbo Cash Out Refinances allow borrowers access up until 85% LTV but only if their homes are valued over $1 million dollars and have been owned for at least two years prior to applying.

## Advantages of a Cash Out Refinance

### Lower Interest Rates

A cash out refinance allows you to take advantage of lower interest rates and a fixed rate mortgage, which can save you money over time.

### Improved Loan Terms

With a cash out refinance, you may be able to obtain better loan terms than what was available when your original home loan was originated. For example, if interest rates have dropped since then or if there has been an improvement in the creditworthiness of the borrower (e.g., good payment history), lenders may allow for an increase in the amount borrowed under this type of financing plan compared with what was originally approved for purchase purposes only.

### Access Additional Funds

With a cash-out refinance loan program, homeowners have access not only their current equity but also any future appreciation they expect while living there - without having any impact on their monthly payments.

### Consolidate Debts

If there are other debts such as student loans, car payments, etc. that might be weighing down upon them financially due either directly or indirectly through higher interest rates than those offered through banks/lenders offering these types services then this option may provide some relief by allowing them pay off those debts faster without having any negative impact on their overall finances.

## Disadvantages of a Cash Out Refinance

### Increased Loan-to-Value Ratio

The main disadvantage is that it can increase your loan-to-value ratio, which means you'll be borrowing more money than before. This can make it harder for you to qualify for other loans in the future. If you're planning on buying another house or car soon, this could be a problem because lenders will look at your total debt load when deciding whether or not they want to give you money.

### Additional Closing Costs

There may be additional closing costs associated with getting approved for a new mortgage loan (especially if no appraisal was done during the first purchase). These fees are typically paid by the borrower but could end up being higher than expected if not planned for ahead of time.

### Potential Tax Implications

There may be potential tax implications from taking out equity in this way--for example: if someone sells their home within two years after buying it as part of an FHA-insured loan program then they must pay back all of those benefits plus 6 percent interest per year on top of that amount until everything has been paid off fully!

## Who Is Eligible for a Cash Out Refinance?

In order to be eligible for a cash out refinance, you need to meet the following requirements:

| Requirement | Minimum |
|-------------|---------|
| Credit Score | 620 |
| Annual Income | $50,000 (or $75,000 if married) |

The loan-to-value ratio (LTV) is another factor that lenders consider when determining whether or not they will approve your request. The LTV is the amount of money being borrowed divided by the value of your home; this means that if you have an 80% LTV on your current mortgage and want to increase it by 10%, then there will be less equity in your home after refinancing.

## How to Prepare for a Cash Out Refinance

Before you begin to shop for a cash out refinance, there are several things you should do:

### Check Your Credit Score

The higher it is, the better. If your score is below 700 and especially if it's below 600, you may want to consider improving it before attempting this type of loan. You can get free access to two of the most popular credit scores at Credit Sesame or Credit Karma; both sites will also show any recent inquiries on your account (which could lower your score) and other important details about how lenders view your finances.

### Review Existing Mortgage Documents

Review existing mortgage documents such as tax forms or homeowner insurance records so that they match up with what lenders will be asking them later when they do their own verification process during underwriting (the process by which they decide whether or not they're willing to lend money). This step is especially important if there have been any changes since getting approved for the original mortgage--for example:

- Moving from one state into another
- Changing jobs
- Getting married/divorced/widowed

### Compare Lenders

There are many different types of companies offering cash out refinance loans so shop around until finding one whose rates fit within budget while still being competitively priced overall compared against others available locally through internet searches.

## What Happens After a Cash Out Refinance?

The first thing that happens after a cash out refinance is that you receive the funds from your new loan. You can use these to pay off any existing debt, or save it for future expenses.

Once your old mortgage is paid off, you'll have access to all of the cash left over in addition to whatever amount was used for paying down other loans or debts. This additional money can be used however you want--to fund investments or make purchases on credit cards (which may come with higher interest rates), pay down student loans or car payments, etc.

The last step of a successful cash out refinance is monitoring your credit score over time so that if there are any problems with late payments or missed payments due to lack of funds coming in from other sources like investments made using profits from selling stocks at market highs.

## Alternatives to a Cash Out Refinance

If you're thinking about a cash out refinance, it's important to know what your alternatives are. Here are some of the most common options:

### Home Equity Loan

With this type of loan, you borrow against the value of your home and use the money for whatever purposes you choose (like paying off debt). You'll have to pay interest on this type of loan just like any other kind; however, there may be tax advantages if you use the funds for something related to improving or repairing your home.

### Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC)

This is similar in many ways to a home equity loan--you get access to funds based on how much equity has been built up in your property--but differs in one key respect: HELOCs require monthly payments while home equity loans typically have fixed monthly payments.

### Second Mortgage or Personal Loan

These options may have different terms and rates compared to a cash out refinance.

### Debt Consolidation Loan

A specialized loan designed to combine multiple debts into a single payment, often at a lower interest rate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between a cash out refinance and a rate and term refinance?

A cash out refinance allows you to borrow more than your current mortgage balance and receive the difference in cash. A rate and term refinance simply replaces your existing mortgage with a new one at different terms, without accessing additional equity.

### What are the tax implications of a cash out refinance?

Interest on the portion of the loan used to buy, build, or substantially improve your home may be tax deductible. However, interest on funds used for other purposes (like debt consolidation) may not be. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

### How long does it take to get approved for a cash out refinance?

The process typically takes 30-45 days from application to closing, though it can vary based on the lender and complexity of your situation.

## Conclusion

A cash out refinance can be a great way to access additional funds, consolidate debts and lower your interest rate. However it is important to understand the risks and costs associated with this type of loan before proceeding.

If you are considering a cash out refinance, it's important that you understand exactly what type of loan you're getting into before signing on the dotted line.
